Isaac Newton

Isaac Newton (1642-1727) was an English mathematician, physicist, and astronomer, widely regarded as one of the most influential scientists in history. He is best known for formulating the three laws of motion, which laid the foundation for classical mechanics and revolutionized our understanding of the physical world. Newton also developed the law of universal gravitation, explaining how objects attract each other based on their masses and distances. His groundbreaking work "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica" (Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy) published in 1687, solidified his status as a scientific pioneer. Beyond physics, Newton made significant contributions to mathematics, optics, and calculus, making him a key figure of the scientific revolution.

  • 1687 :

    The Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy (Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica) - Laid the foundation for classical mechanics and introduced Newton's laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation.
  • 1704 :

    Opticks: Or, a Treatise of the Reflexions, Refractions, Inflexions, and Colours of Light - Explored the nature of light and color, contributing to the understanding of optics and the behavior of light.
  • 1728 :

    The Chronology of Ancient Kingdoms Amended - Investigated historical chronologies and attempted to correct the dating of ancient events.
  • 1736 :

    Method of Fluxions - Developed calculus, though not widely known during his lifetime.
